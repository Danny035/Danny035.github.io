This chart displays the number of reported crashes per 10,000 residents across three cities — Boston, Cambridge, and Somerville. To account for population size differences and allow for fair comparison, we normalized the total crash counts using census population data. Cambridge shows the highest crash rate at 904.9 per 10,000 people, followed by Somerville (604.2), and Boston (508.4). This suggests that while Boston may have more total crashes due to its size, residents in Cambridge experience crashes at a significantly higher rate relative to the population.

This interactive pie chart visualization shows how vehicle crashes are distributed over different times of day, and lets users explore the data in more detail. The left chart displays crash counts across four major time periods: Morning, Afternoon, Evening, and Midnight. When a user clicks on a slice of this chart, the right chart automatically updates, showing an hourly breakdown for that selected period. This interactivity helps users drill down into patterns and identify specific hours when crashes are most likely to occur.

Stack Bar Plot

This interactive bar chart illustrates the distribution of vehicle crashes across Boston, Cambridge, and Somerville, categorized by injury severity and normalized by population to ensure fair comparison. Injury types were simplified into four main categories—No Injury, Minor Injury, Serious Injury, and Fatal—to reduce redundancy and improve clarity. By adjusting for each city's population (using crashes per 10,000 residents), the chart highlights differences in crash outcomes that aren’t immediately visible through raw counts alone. Users can filter the chart by severity level to explore how the frequency and severity of crashes vary across the three cities, offering insight into relative safety conditions in each area.

Crash Flow by City and Severity

This Sankey diagram helps reveal how collision type outcomes differ by city not just in frequency but in severity distribution and can be filtered by other collision types using the dropdown. It’s especially useful for identifying whether certain cities experience more severe outcomes for specific crash types, which can inform targeted safety interventions.

Heatmap: Crash Severity by Weather Condition

This heatmap visualizes the relationship between weather conditions and crash severity, with color intensity representing the number of crashes. The darker the cell, the higher the crash count. The chart is also filterable by city, enabling more localized insights. Crashes in clear weather dominate across all cities especially those involving property damage only. This suggests most driving (and crashing) happens in normal conditions. Cambridge shows relatively higher crash activity in cloudy and rainy conditions compared to Boston and Somerville, indicating weather might have more influence there. Snow and icy conditions lead to a more even distribution of crash severity (including non-fatal injuries) in Somerville, suggesting that winter conditions may present greater risk in that city.
